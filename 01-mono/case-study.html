Overview

Pulse is the analytics surface our customers open first thing every morning. The previous version polled the server every few seconds and buckled under heavy traffic — charts lagged, tabs froze, and the data was always a little stale. I rebuilt it from the streaming layer up.

This is mock copy you can replace with your own narrative. The structure below — problem, role, process, solution, results — works for most engineering case studies.

The problem

  • Polling architecture couldn't keep up past ~500 concurrent dashboards.
  • Each client re-fetched the full dataset, wasting bandwidth and CPU.
  • Rendering 50k points per chart locked the main thread for seconds.
  • No backpressure meant traffic spikes cascaded into outages.

My role

I led architecture and owned the streaming pipeline and the rendering layer. I paired with two engineers on the aggregation service and worked with design on progressive-disclosure patterns for dense data.

Process & approach

We replaced polling with a WebSocket fan-out backed by a Go aggregation service. Events are pre-bucketed server-side so the client only ever receives deltas.

// streaming contract — server pushes deltas only
ws.subscribe("metrics.cpu", { window: "1m" })
  .on("delta", applyToRingBuffer)
  .on("snapshot", rehydrate)

On the client, charts render to an offscreen canvas via a worker, so the main thread stays free for interaction even during traffic bursts.

The solution

  • WebSocket delta streaming with server-side bucketing.
  • Ring-buffer state with a worker-driven canvas renderer.
  • Adaptive sampling that degrades gracefully under load.
  • Backpressure and reconnection baked into the client SDK.

Results

Pulse now serves thousands of concurrent dashboards on a fraction of the previous infrastructure. Support tickets about lag dropped to nearly zero, and the streaming SDK was extracted into an internal package used by three other teams.
